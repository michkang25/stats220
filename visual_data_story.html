Introduction

In this analysis, we explore how bus occupancy is influenced by various factors, such as punctuality and the activities passengers are engaged in. Well look at data from bus journeys to uncover patterns in the number of passengers and how they interact with the bus environment.

To better understand passenger behavior, we analyzed responses to the question: What was the most common activity people were doing on the bus? Activities were grouped into broader categories using text pattern recognition techniques.

The bar chart below shows that phone-related activities (e.g., texting, scrolling, or making calls) are the most common. This is followed by inactive behavior such as sitting quietly or staring out the window, and socializing through talking or chatting. This suggests that while many passengers are engaged with their devices, a significant portion remain passive or interact with others.

Understanding what people commonly do while commuting on the bus connects to broader social and urban trends. For example:

Digital device use (on their phone) reflects our increasingly mobile and connected lifestyles. It suggests passengers are using commute time to check messages, scroll social media, or catch up on work or entertainment.

Looking out the window or sleeping may indicate that some passengers use the time to rest or unwind, highlighting how public transport can be a moment of pause in busy urban routines.

Social interactions like talking to someone hint at how public spaces like buses can still foster real-world connection, despite the rise in digital engagement.

From a public transport planning perspective, knowing how people spend their time on the bus can inform decisions around:

Improving ride comfort (e.g., stable Wi-Fi, better seating)

Reducing crowding for a more relaxing journey

Promoting digital services or health messaging that passengers can engage with during travel
